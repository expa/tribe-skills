# Tribe AI — Voice & Style Analysis

Distilled from 17 published articles (`articles-text.json`) and full website CMS copy (`site-copy.json`, legal pages excluded). Two registers: **Website voice** (headlines, section copy, CTAs, microcopy) and **Article voice** (long-form editorial). They share one brand DNA but behave differently.

---

## Register A: Website Voice

### Headline patterns

- **Sentence case, terminal period — always.** Even 3-word headlines get a full stop: "Notes from the field." / "It takes a Tribe." / "Build at the frontier." The period signals conviction: these are statements, not labels.
- **Short.** Hero and section titles run 3–9 words. The longest ("We embed. We build. We don't stop until it's in production.") is really three staccato sentences.
- **Two dominant structures:**
  1. *Staccato triad* — "We embed. We build. We don't stop until it's in production." / "AI that ships, scales, and compounds."
  2. *Contrast pivot* — "Most companies have the tools, not the results." / "What separates the companies pulling ahead isn't the model. It's the people around it."
- **Aphorism positioning lines** on industry pages: "Intelligence for the physical world." / "Personal at the scale of every customer." — compressed, almost poster-like.
- **No question headlines. No exclamation marks. No Title Case.** (Title Case survives only in a few CTA button labels like "Start a Conversation" — inconsistently; sentence case dominates.)

### Eyebrow / label conventions

Short noun phrases, sentence case, no punctuation: "Insights", "How we work", "From the inside", "Candidate experience", "Your impact at Tribe", "Careers at Tribe", "Start a conversation". Eyebrows name the section plainly; the headline underneath carries the personality.

### Body copy rhythm

- One to three sentences per block. A longer scene-setting sentence followed by a short punch: "A quarter here feels like a year anywhere else. … The learning compounds, fast."
- **Em dash as the signature move** — a mid-sentence pivot that lands the point: "We work directly with the labs building today's best models — and engineer the systems that make them work at enterprise scale." / "Your data — and the AI we build on it — stays inside your walls."
- Industry-page formula: *[audience] + [pain rooted in data or manual work] + "Tribe helps [X] deploy AI that [concrete outcomes]"*. E.g., "Banks, insurers, fintechs, and investment firms sit on massive amounts of data but still rely on manual workflows… Tribe helps financial organizations deploy AI that improves underwriting, automates operations, reduces fraud…"

### CTA verb style

Invitational and conversational, never salesy: "Start a conversation", "View open roles", "Join us", "Come build with us", "Tell us where you're headed.", "Back to home". The primary CTA across the whole site is literally a conversation, not a demo. No "Book a demo", "Request a consultation", "Get started free", "Learn more" (as a primary CTA).

### How they talk about AI, clients, talent

- **AI is a tool, never magic.** The models are commodity ("When every company can call the same APIs and get the same models"); the differentiation is deployment and people.
- **Clients are the world's largest/most complex companies** — described by scale and difficulty, not flattery: "inside the world's most complex companies", "the largest companies in the world".
- **Talent = "builders."** Never "resources", "consultants", or "staff". "From our builders, in their own words." Team pages celebrate provenance (NASA, Google, OpenAI) without adjectives.

### Person and address

- Relentless first-person plural: "We embed. We build." "We sit with the teams we serve."
- Second person for the reader's stake: "You'll work alongside the leading AI labs…", "You start running on day one, not after a six-month ramp."
- The pattern is often *we do X → you get Y* inside one sentence: "We give you a real problem and you'll own it all the way into production."

### Named voice attributes (Website)

1. **Declarative confidence** — statements end in periods, even headlines; no questions, no maybes, no exclamation points.
2. **Compression** — every idea reduced to its fewest words; triads and parallel verbs carry the rhythm ("ships, scales, and compounds").
3. **Contrast framing** — meaning built by negation and pivot: "not the results", "isn't the model. It's the people.", "Low ego, high bar", "real, not hypothetical", "high signal, low noise".
4. **Production obsession** — the destination of every promise is production, not strategy: "We don't stop until it's in production."
5. **Human warmth in the machine** — microcopy stays human and lightly playful where stakes are low: "We say hello", "This page lost its momentum. Give the cradle a swing — then head back home."
6. **Plainspoken anti-consulting** — no frameworks, no jargon, no superlatives; reads like an operator wrote it, not a marketer.
7. **Earned specificity** — numbers and proper nouns instead of adjectives: "45 minutes", "18,000+ vendors", "SOC 2 Type II certified".

### DO / DON'T (Website)

- **DO** end headlines with a period. **DON'T** use Title Case or exclamation marks in headlines.
- **DO** write "We build AI that makes it to production." **DON'T** write "We deliver innovative AI solutions."
- **DO** use the contrast pivot: "Most companies have the tools, not the results." **DON'T** open with "In today's fast-paced world…"
- **DO** invite: "Start a conversation." **DON'T** push: "Book a demo now!"
- **DO** name the client's pain concretely ("still rely on manual workflows, fragmented systems, and slow decision-making"). **DON'T** flatter vaguely ("industry-leading organizations like yours").
- **DO** use em dashes for the pivot that lands the point. **DON'T** stack subordinate clauses; keep sentences ≤ ~20 words.
- **DO** say "builders", "engineers", "talent". **DON'T** say "resources", "consultants", "experts on demand".
- **DO** let low-stakes microcopy be playful (404, hiring steps). **DON'T** joke in high-stakes copy (security, compliance, client outcomes).

### Vocabulary (recurring) and anti-patterns

**They actually say:** production; ship; deploy / forward-deployed; embed; build / builders; frontier; compound / compounding; unlock; real (real systems, real signal, real work, "make it real"); own it end to end; the largest companies in the world; institutional knowledge; judgment; enterprise; talent; bar (high bar, "that's the bar"); moat-adjacent phrasing like "an advantage nobody can copy".

**Notably absent (anti-patterns):** cutting-edge, revolutionary, game-changing, next-generation, seamless*, best-in-class, world-class, empower, harness, journey, synergy, digital transformation, "AI-powered solutions", holistic, turnkey, "unlock the power of AI" (they say *unlock* but always with a concrete object). No exclamation points anywhere on the site. No emoji. No questions as headlines. (*"seamless/seamlessly" appears only in legacy case-study body text, not in the new brand-register copy.)

### Rewrite demonstration (Website)

- **Generic:** "We leverage cutting-edge AI solutions to drive digital transformation for enterprises across industries."
- **Tribe:** "Every enterprise has access to the same models. We build the systems — and the teams — that turn them into production. That's the difference."
- Or headline-length: "The models are everyone's. The results are yours."

## Register B: Article Voice

### Structure conventions

**Openings — no throat-clearing.** Articles start inside the argument or inside a scene, first sentence:

- Scene: "It's 9:47 AM on a Tuesday. Your AI assistant helpfully summarizes the quarterly sales report you requested. What you don't see: it just sent your entire customer database to a server in Eastern Europe." (securing-mcps)
- News-thesis: "Last week, OpenAI and Anthropic both announced deployment companies. … The pattern is unmistakable." (why-we-bought-a-recruiting-firm)
- Personal stake: "When Jackie and I started building Tribe back in 2019, we made two bets:" (introducing-the-new-tribe)
- Research articles open with a literal "## TL;DR"; technical guides may open with "## Executive summary" and a numbered "This article outlines:" map.

**Section headings.** H2s in narrative pieces are full thoughts, often thesis fragments: "The human is the unlock", "Ambition without apology", "Technical background barely mattered", "Sharing was the weakest dimension in the company". Technical/announcement pieces use functional Title Case: "How It Works", "Why This Collaboration Matters", "The Context Window Challenge". H3s carry numbered frameworks ("1. Bridging Vision & Execution").

**Closings.** Articles land on a compressed restatement plus an invitation. The final beat is usually 1–3 very short sentences: "That's the unlock. That's Tribe." / "We're here to make AI real." CTAs are direct and often emoji-pointed: "👉 View open roles at Tribe", "👉 Tribe is hiring… come build with us", "Get in touch", "Try Brand Voice →". Founder letters may sign off personally: "— Jackie & Noah", "Here's to the next six!"

### Title patterns by category

- **Announcements:** plain declarative, founder-personal, often sentence case: "Why we bought a recruiting firm", "Tribe's First Fundraise", "Introducing the New Tribe: The human is the unlock". Colon subtitles carry the thesis.
- **Research:** the finding as a sentence, first person, period included: "We measured every person at our company on AI. Here's what we learned."
- **GenAI (practitioner):** Topic: Benefit colon structure, Title Case: "Legacy Code Modernization with Claude Code: Breaking Through Context Window Barriers", "OpenAI's New Realtime API Updates: Enterprise Applications & Impact".
- **Anthropic (partnership/product):** colon titles with a dramatized or branded hook: "Securing MCPs for the Autonomous Age: Your AI Assistant Just Leaked Your Customer Database", "Claude at Scale: Optimizing Cost & Performance for Enterprise AI", "Your Brand, at Scale: Introducing Brand Voice for Claude Cowork".

### Tone, data, hedging, humor

- **Confident and declarative**, mirroring the site: "The pattern is unmistakable." "This isn't fiction." "Research that doesn't change behavior is just content."
- **Data-saturated.** Real numbers everywhere, often uncomfortable ones: "63% of people who built working AI tools had never deployed anything beyond their own laptop." Costs are computed to the dollar ("$3,960,000 → $1,370,547 — a 65% reduction"). External stats get sources ("(Businesswire)", footnote "¹ Equixly").
- **Hedging is replaced by honest admission.** They almost never write "may", "might", "could potentially" about their own claims. Instead, credibility comes from confessing errors: "We underestimated how hard it would be to make companies care. We overestimated how quickly the tooling would mature." "We realized we had the same blind spot as everyone else."
- **Humor is dry and rare** — a wink, not a joke: "some are even optimizing 'tokenmaxxing'", "even for fixing simple apostrophe errors in convoluted cURL commands", "We did the Santa Cruz things." Announcements allow one exclamation of genuine excitement ("we're hiring!", "(!)").
- **Concept-coining.** They name their ideas so they travel: "the 80% cliff", "the three cliffs", "the human is the unlock", "horsepower over pedigree", "practitioners not consultants, builders not talkers", "forward deployed engineers".

### Formatting habits

- Bulleted lists with **bolded lead-in phrases** followed by an em dash or colon: "Perfectionism — 'It's not ready yet.'"
- Numbered frameworks (three phases, six dimensions, four layers) — nearly every piece has one.
- Pull quotes (blockquotes) used for their own findings and for customer/partner voices: "'The tools were there. The talent was there. The connective tissue wasn't.'"
- Named partner quotes with full attribution: "– Jaclyn Rice Nelson, Co-founder & CEO, Tribe AI".
- Paragraphs are 2–4 sentences; single-sentence paragraphs used as beats ("So we acquired our recruiting partner, Candor.").
- Rhetorical question → immediate answer: "The better question is: is the knowledge compounding?"

### How they reference clients and partners

- **Clients are anonymized but concretely drawn:** "a global consulting firm renowned for its leadership in corporate turnarounds", "a large-scale membership-based travel business". Never named, always given texture and numbers.
- **Partners are named proudly and often:** OpenAI, Anthropic, AWS, Google — with claimed proximity: "we see what's coming before it's public", "I've had the chance to sit in the room at OpenAI and Anthropic."
- **The customer's voice is used as the bar:** "'What I want from you is to act like my problems are your problems.' That's the bar."

### Named voice attributes (Article)

1. **Thesis-first** — the argument arrives in the first two sentences; context comes after, never before.
2. **Operator's evidence** — every claim is backed by a number, a named system, or a first-hand scene; adjectives are not evidence.
3. **Confession over hedging** — certainty about the thesis, candor about mistakes; "we got plenty wrong" replaces "results may vary".
4. **Named ideas** — findings get memorable handles ("the 80% cliff") so readers can carry them out of the article.
5. **Beat-drop rhythm** — long explanatory passages resolved by one- or two-word sentences: "That's the bar." "No vibes."
6. **Builder's generosity** — how-to sections hand over the actual playbook (prompts, queries, frameworks): "How to run this yourself."
7. **Founder intimacy (Announcements only)** — first names, personal history, gratitude; "Jackie and I", "— Jackie & Noah".
8. **Frontier positioning** — recurring claim of being early and inside: "since 2019", "before ChatGPT even existed", "in the room where the next generation of AI is being defined".

### DO / DON'T (Article)

- **DO** open with the thesis or a scene: "Last week, OpenAI and Anthropic both announced deployment companies." **DON'T** open with "In recent years, artificial intelligence has become increasingly important."
- **DO** give exact numbers and their source: "43% of tested MCP implementations contain command injection vulnerabilities¹". **DON'T** write "many implementations have security issues."
- **DO** admit what went wrong: "Those early years were painful and we were almost too early." **DON'T** hedge the thesis: "AI may potentially transform some businesses."
- **DO** coin a name for the finding ("the deployment cliff"). **DON'T** leave insights as unlabeled paragraphs.
- **DO** end sections with a short beat: "That's the unlock. That's Tribe." **DON'T** end with a summary paragraph that restates everything.
- **DO** structure with numbered frameworks and bold-lead bullets. **DON'T** write walls of prose longer than 4 sentences per paragraph.
- **DO** anonymize clients but keep them vivid ("a global consulting firm renowned for…"). **DON'T** name clients or reduce them to "a Fortune 500 company we worked with" without texture.
- **DO** close with a direct invitation ("👉 Tribe is hiring", "Get in touch"). **DON'T** close with "Contact us today to learn how we can help your business succeed!"

### Vocabulary (recurring) and anti-patterns

**They actually say:** production ("in production", "production-ready", "production-grade"); forward deployed; enterprise / the enterprise; frontier (labs, models, partnerships, "at the frontier"); unlock; compound / compounding; bets ("we made two bets", "bold bets"); inflection point; early innings / first inning; ROI, measurable results, outcomes vs. output; pattern recognition; institutional knowledge; judgment; talent; builders; "move fast and build with confidence"; "make AI real"; ship; the gap ("bridge the gap", "closing the gap between model intelligence and business value").

**Notably absent (anti-patterns):** "delve", "landscape" (as in "AI landscape"), "in today's world", "game-changer", "paradigm shift", "synergies", "robust solutions" as filler, "thought leadership", "utilize", "best practices" as a crutch, breathless hype ("mind-blowing", "insane"), and fear-mongering about AI replacing people (their line is always the opposite: "AI doesn't transform a company. People do."). They never trash-talk competitors by name — the anti-consultant jab is structural ("strategy decks wouldn't move the needle"), not personal. Minimal hedging vocabulary: "arguably", "perhaps", "it could be said" essentially never appear.

### Rewrite demonstration (Article)

- **Generic:** "In today's rapidly evolving AI landscape, organizations must embrace digital transformation to remain competitive, though results may vary depending on implementation."
- **Tribe:** "Every company is buying the same models. Almost none are getting results. The gap isn't the technology — it's the people who can put it to work inside the enterprise. We've watched this play out across hundreds of deployments since 2019, and the pattern is unmistakable."

## Shared DNA (both registers)

1. **The people thesis.** Every piece, site or article, resolves to the same belief: models are commodity; humans — judgment, talent, deployment — are the unlock. "AI doesn't transform a company. People do."
2. **Production as the finish line.** Success is never "strategy", "insights", or "enablement" — it's software running in production and staying there.
3. **Contrast construction.** The signature syntactic move everywhere: "isn't X — it's Y", "not A, but B", "tools, not results", "outcomes rather than output", "practitioners not consultants".
4. **Specificity over adjectives.** Numbers, named systems, real timelines do the persuading; superlatives are almost entirely absent.
5. **Confidence without hype.** Declarative sentences, short beats, no hedging — but also no hype vocabulary. The tone is an experienced operator, calm because they've done it.

## Key differences

1. **Compression vs. expansion.** Website copy compresses the thesis to aphorisms ("It takes a Tribe."); articles expand it with evidence, frameworks, and narrative.
2. **Person and intimacy.** The site is corporate-we + you; articles admit "I", first names ("Jackie and I"), personal history, and signed closings — especially Announcements.
3. **Punctuation license.** The site permits no exclamation marks or emoji, ever; articles allow a rare celebratory "we're hiring!" and the 👉 CTA pointer.
4. **Playfulness placement.** On the site, humor lives only in microcopy (404, hiring steps); in articles it appears as dry asides inside serious arguments ("tokenmaxxing", "We did the Santa Cruz things").
5. **Time.** Website copy is timeless present tense (what Tribe is and does); articles are anchored in moments ("Last week…", "It's 9:47 AM on a Tuesday", "earlier this month") and in Tribe's own timeline since 2019.
6. **Heading grammar.** Site headlines are sentence-case statements with periods; article H2s split by genre — thesis fragments in narrative pieces, functional Title Case in technical guides.
